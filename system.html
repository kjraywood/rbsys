3.3. Vs double of Stayman

In order of priority:
 ●  3♣ = 4-4 majors max  ●  2NT = 4-4 majors min
 ●  pass promises a stopper   (may have a stack)

3.3.1. When opener passes

Responder can redouble as re‑Stayman, but must have at least two clubs and at least invitational values because opener might have a stack and be intending to pass.

With a garbage-Stayman hand (three-suiter short in clubs), responder bids 2♦ and opener corrects with a four-card major.

With short-clubs and a GF, responder bids 3♣ as re‑Stayman. Smolen is ON.

Other bids are as though opener had replied to Stayman with 2♦, although in this case, opener may still have a four-card major. It’s possible to miss a 4‑-4 major-suit fit when responder rebids 2NT and opener passes.

6.1. Inverted Minors

Single-raise = game-forcing

With 5-cards or more in the major, we raise the minor in preference to bidding the four-card major. In essence, a single raise is a 2/1 in partner’s minor, and the auction proceeds just as after any other 2/1.

We bid our longest suit first with a game force so that:
 ●  we bid shape more accurately,   enabling more accurate   hand evaluation
 ●  1m-1M auctions are clearer
 ●  establishing a fit and GF   prepares us better for   third-hand intervention

36.2. The aggressive shift

  Updated Dec 4, 2018

We give attitude on opening lead, even in a suit-contract with shortness (s|v) in dummy. A discouraging signal implies that we can stand the aggressive shift; usually to dummy’s weakest side-suit.

We identify the aggressive shift with the following rules in order of priority.

36.2.1. Negatives

  • not trumps

  • not a suit headed by AKQ, KQJ, or AKJT

  • not a singleton or void in a suit-contract except it could be a singleton vs a slam

  • not a suit that was shown by declarer

36.2.2. Positives

  • opening-leader’s suit (when a different suit was led)

  • suit shown by leader’s partner (when a different suit was led)

  • vs suit: a 3-card suit headed by at most one honour

  • vs suit: if no weak-three-card suit, then shortest side-suit but not a singleton or void (excluded by negative)

  • vs notrump: shortest side-suit not excluded by a negative

36.2.3. Exceptions

  • Q from AKQ vs suit ➤  count

  • K from AK vs suit-slam ➤  count

  • trump lead ➤  suit preference

  • vs suits on the lead of K (from AK) and dummy holds four or more to the J or T ➤  simple attitude

  • vs suits: partner leads K (from AK), dummy has a stiff, long trumps, and equal-length short-suits ➤  suit-preference

  • vs NT on a spot-card lead, if dummy is winning the trick holding Q-J-x or J-T-x, we give count, followed by suit-preference when declarer plays to trick two. This also applies if dummy has Q-T-x, provided declarer wins the ten.

If we cannot have a significant honour (J or higher) we give count

A ten is usually treated as an honour card (it might win a trick), but when partner leads a king (from AKx) and dummy has the guarded queen, we treat the ten like any other spot card. We would play it from Txx to discourage a continuation because we can’t ruff the third round.

36.2.4. Problem hands

In third seat when you don’t want the suit led, and you don’t want the aggressive shift, signal for the suit that you think is least likely to cost a trick.

36.3. Honour sequences

  Following suit from honour sequences     –  Ed Zaluski

When partner leads low and we have an honour sequence, we usually play 2nd-highest . However, if we know that lowest of the sequence will make our holding known to partner, then that’s the card we play.

2nd highest reveals declarer’s high honours to partner.

36.4. Discards

Natural discards have priority. These are:
 ●  top of honour sequence
 ●  2nd-last in partner’s suit
 ●  from a five-card suit in a   4-card suit of dummy or declarer

First spot-card discard is upside-down attitude unless it is in dummy’s long-suit and partner needs count. Subsequent discards in the same suit are suit preference.

If declarer has shown out of a suit, discards of that suit are suit-preference.

We discard immediately from a suit we cannot guard; unless it is declarer’s guess suit.

If we have a suit that is ready to run, then an immediate encouraging discard from a side suit, shows a quick entry in that suit.

If we lead a suit against NT, declarer wins and runs a suit forcing discards from us, including discards from the suit we have led, then:
 ●  an early discard from our led suit   = our suit is not ready to run  ●  a late discard from our led suit   = our suit is ready to run

36.5. Suit-Preference Returns

When playing a suit for partner to ruff and dummy holds no honour-card:
 ●  Q or T = high-suit pref.  ●  J = low-suit pref.

36.6. Getting a Ruff

When you intend to shift to a singleton and hold AKx in the led suit, win the first trick with the ace.

By making it appear that declarer has the king, you discourage other lines of defence.
